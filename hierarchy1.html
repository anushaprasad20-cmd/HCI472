Hierarchy 01 · Two Typefaces

Editorial Luxe.

A pairing study — a contemporary variable serif with softened terminals in conversation with a humanist technical sans. Warm cream stock, inkwell navy, a whisper of burgundy. The magazine re-imagined for the screen.

Typefaces — Fraunces · IBM Plex Sans Scale — 1.333 Perfect Fourth Base — 16px
Display · H1 67px · 1.333⁵
Fraunces
400 · Italic
opsz 144 · SOFT 30

The quiet authority of a well-set display line.

A display heading is a promise — the first note struck before the argument begins. Here, Fraunces drops to its lightest weight, lifts its optical size to 144, and leans softly into italic; the result is voice before volume. Read more about Fraunces' variable axes in the specimen below.

Section · H2 51px · 1.333⁴
Fraunces
500 · Roman
opsz 96 · SOFT 20

On the architecture of the page.

Section headings organize thought. A slightly heavier weight and a reduced optical size give H2 structural presence without competing with the display line above it. The transition from step five to step four of the modular scale is perceptible but harmonious — a perfect fourth, after all, is a musical interval built for resolution.

Subsection · H3 38px · 1.333³
Fraunces
600 · Semibold
opsz 48

Pairing serif with sans — a brief treatise.

At H3 the serif tightens its tracking and settles into text-optical-size proportions. It still reads as display but begins to feel like running type. Beneath it, IBM Plex Sans — Plex's humanist shapes and slightly mechanical spirit form a natural counterweight to Fraunces' warmth.

Minor · H4 28px · 1.333²
Fraunces
600 · Semibold
opsz 24

The smallest heading still has a job.

H4 is often the most overlooked level in a hierarchy — asked to introduce without interrupting. Here, a compact optical size keeps the serif contrast moderate, and the weight steps up to semibold so the heading still holds attention against body copy only 12 pixels smaller.

Body Text 16px · 1.333⁰
IBM Plex Sans
400 · Regular
line-height 1.65

The body is where the reader lives. In this hierarchy, Plex Sans carries every paragraph — set at sixteen pixels with a generous line-height of 1.65, measure capped at sixty-two characters. Nothing shouts. Plex's slightly squared counters and gently open apertures give long text a steady cadence, the kind of rhythm that disappears into the act of reading itself. A well-tuned body text is an act of hospitality — the designer stepping out of the room so the writer can speak.

Emphasis 16px · Italic
Fraunces
500 · Italic
SOFT 50

Emphasis is a deliberate interruption. In this pairing, emphasized text leaves Plex and briefly adopts Fraunces italic — a one-step tonal shift that makes the break feel authored rather than accidental. A burgundy color token, used sparingly, carries the eye through a paragraph without fragmenting it. Restraint is the entire technique; used on every third word, emphasis collapses into noise.

Link Text 16px · Medium
IBM Plex Sans
500 · Regular
1px underline

Links stay within the Plex family so the reading surface remains unbroken. A medium weight, a burgundy fill, and a hairline underline signal interactivity without flagging it. On hover, the relationship inverts: the link fills with burgundy and the text lifts to cream — a brief, confident commitment. Try it here: this is how a link should behave. The feedback is immediate, the motion is zero — just color doing exactly what color is good at.

Scale: 1.333 — the Perfect Fourth.

Each size is derived by multiplying the previous by 1.333, starting from a 16 px base. The ratio borrows its name from music, where the perfect fourth is the interval between the first and fourth notes of a major scale — spacious, consonant, and quietly structural. Classical in spirit, which suits the editorial temperament of this pairing.
